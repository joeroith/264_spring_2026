Catalog course description:

After mastering the foundations of data science – especially data wrangling and visualization – in SDS164, data science students are ready to explore deeper fundamentals, impactful applications, and project workflow. Computational fundamentals may include iteration, functions, conditions, data types, SQL, regular expressions, version control, and simulation – all in the context of real data. New applications may include text as data, personal webpages, and web scraping to produce dynamic graphics. These fundamentals and applications will be presented with attention to data ethics, reproducible research, quality code, and sound workflow practices.

Extended course description:

Data science is a relatively new field that sits at the intersection between statistics and computer science. At its heart, data science is about gleaning information and making decisions from data. Knowledge of how to organize, visualize, and analyze data has never been a more important or marketable skill. Data is the currency of the modern world; huge declines in the costs of computing and computer storage mean that companies like Google and Amazon, as well as scientists and policy makers, have access to more data than the world has ever seen. Data science provides tools for gaining insight and understanding of problems in fields as diverse as economics, psychology, medicine, sociology, political science, etc.

In this course, we will build on the fundamentals of data wrangling and data visualization that you gained in Data Science 1. There are still many fundamentals of literate programming that a modern data scientist should possess, and we will focus on many of those fundamentals in the presence of real data and real questions. Examples include writing functions (to perform common tasks efficiently), iteration (to effectively loop through similar instances), regular expressions (to clean and analyze text data), web scraping (to acquire analyzable data from websites and other sources), version control (to work well in group settings), and structured query language (SQL – to wrangle even larger data sets). But then we will take these newly mastered fundamentals and apply them to several areas of application, including mapping, webpage creation, and dynamic graphics and dashboards!

This course, like Data Science 1, is not a substitute for an introduction to statistics, where you would study core ideas like study design, variability, and inference at a deeper level, nor is it a substitute for computer science, where you would study algorithms and systems at a deeper level. But hopefully this course will provide you with an even fuller set of tools for discovery and decision making with data.

Course objectives:

Students completing Data Science 2 will be able to:

  1. Create meaningful choropleth maps

  2. Use efficient and literate coding practices such as iteration, functions, and regular expressions

  3. Write simulations to investigate properties of data

  4. Extract data from databases using structured query language

  5. Scrape data from websites and other sources to create analyzable data sets

  6. Produce dynamic graphs that a user can interact with

  7. Work in data science teams using version control and other sound workflow practices

  8. Tell stories with data while carefully considering ethical implications

Additionally, by the end of SDS 264, I hope that each of you has:

  1. Developed a skill set and at least one tangible product that would impress a future interviewer

  2. Taught me at least one new thing about data science

Features of this course

Philosophy: This course is centered on the idea that you will better understand and retain important ideas from data science if you practice using them, rather than by memorizing and regurgitating a set of facts. In order to actively construct knowledge in data science, you must:

  • engage in the material and actively code. Just try stuff, even if it doesn’t work – you can’t break R!

  • attend class, participate in class, and complete pre-class preparations.

Homework: Regular practice with this material is critical! Homework assignments will primarily involve sets of questions that we begin together in class and which you finish on your own. Some important notes on homework assignments:

  • Homework assignments may be done in collaboration with other students (this is highly encouraged). However, the final product must be written by you, in your own words. This applies to R code too – share ideas but write your own code. In no event can you copy or paraphrase answers from another student, a web site (including ChatGPT and other LLM tools), solutions manuals, or elsewhere (see Academic Integrity policy below).

  • Homework will be assessed according to the following scale:

    • Check plus (Excellent in Moodle)= everything is completed, and the work is thorough and insightful (100)

    • Check (Adequate) = everything is completed, but at a basic level with a few errors (85)

    • Check minus (Inadequate) = work is partially complete and/or has many errors (70)

    • No credit (Missing) = nothing is submitted (so submit something!!) (0)

  • Anything handed in after the due date and time will be assessed a late penalty, unless cleared well in advance. Anything handed in once the key is posted (usually within 24 hours of the due date) can receive no more than a Check without prior approval. If you must turn in an assignment late, please send it directly to me, and if you have questions about how something was graded, please check with me. In no circumstance should you contact our TA directly about late HWs or how a HW was graded.

  • Homework is completed using Quarto and you are required to upload knitted pdf files to Moodle. Be sure to allow time for rendering and check that the rendered pdf is complete. You must also save the .qmd file that generated the pdf in your Submit folder.

R Tip of the Day: With so many cool new R packages and features coming online daily, it’s hard to keep up! Thus, each student will have a day in which they present the “R Tip of the Day”. You will give a 4-5 minute presentation about the R package or function, what it does, and provide a few examples of new R code in action with real data. You will also use cool presentation features in Quarto.

Knowledge Quizzes in Moodle: For a few topics, rather than a Mini-project, we will have a short quiz to check your understanding of key ideas and how they might be applied. Some of these Knowledge Quizzes will be conducted during class time, while others will be given in take-home format with restrictions on outside materials that can be used. The take-home quizzes will typically involve an oral component in which we can discuss your responses.

Projects: Key products from this course will be organized into 3 mini-projects and 1 final project. The projects will allow you to use concepts from class in larger, more realistic applications where you will have some creative license in your topic choices. Some projects will be completed individually and others in small groups. No projects will be accepted after the final due date. Mini-project 2 will be conducted in pairs (assigned by me, with your input); it will consist of both an R product and an individual oral assessment in which you talk me through your group’s code. No projects will be accepted after the final due date.

Computing: The software package, R, will be used for this course. It is being used in more and more companies (e.g., Google, Mayo Clinic, General Mills) and more and more disciplines (e.g., biology, psychology, political science) for sophisticated data analysis and visualization. R and RStudio are freely available to run on any platform; in fact, I will ask everyone to download their own personal copies of R and RStudio and perform work there. That way, we can use the latest and greatest package versions, integration with GitHub is easier, and you’ll be set up nicely for data science activities beyond St. Olaf.

Academic Integrity: Plagiarism, the unacknowledged appropriation of another person’s words or ideas, is a serious academic offense. It is imperative that you hand in work that is your own, and that cites or gives credit to others whenever you draw from their work. Please see St. Olaf’s statements on academic integrity and plagiarism. See also the description of St. Olaf’s honor system.

St. Olaf’s Academic Integrity Policy, including the Honor System, is an integral part of your academic experience. I consider any violation of this code to be extremely serious and will handle each case appropriately. Here are some guidelines for this class. They do not cover all eventualities so if you have any doubts about a course of action you can ask me.

  • In no event can you copy or paraphrase answers or R code from another student, a web site, solutions manuals, or elsewhere.

  • When you sign your pledge on an exam or project that you have “neither given nor received assistance, and seen no dishonest work” I treat your signature as your solemn pledge that all your actions have been honorable.

  • Don’t treat the honor code lightly; if you’re in doubt about a possible violation, ask me.

The use of AI (e.g., ChatGPT and other large language models) creates brand new challenges in academic integrity. On one hand, many jobs will want you to strategically use AI to enhance your productivity, and there are tasks it does extremely well (summarize information in a digestible manner, answer detailed coding questions much faster than you skimming multiple webpages for the right solution, etc.). On the other hand, it can confidently give you incorrect responses in addition to the ethical concerns with energy use, mining confidential data, etc. The bottom line for me is that in order for you to diagnose the inevitable coding and descriptive error produced by AI, you must put in the work to become expert enough in a particular error to recognize unhelpful responses, and that expertise only comes from putting in the work first without leaning on the AI crutch.

Therefore, in this course, I will specify assignments when AI is NOT allowed (to help you build expertise) and assignments when AI is allowed if properly attributed (to help you enhance your final products). I may ask for your input in setting some of these policies. If restrictions are ever unclear, please ask!
